## Design Context

### Users
Primary users are casual friend groups playing Diplomacy together. The product is used mostly for quick phone check-ins during an ongoing game, with desktop sessions as a secondary context for longer planning, map review, and adjudication. The core job is to let players re-enter the game state quickly, understand the board without friction, coordinate private strategic thinking, and submit decisions with confidence from a small screen.

### Brand Personality
The brand should feel serious, atmospheric, and direct. It should evoke the tension of historical statecraft rather than the polish of a generic modern SaaS tool, but the wording should stay operational instead of theatrical or promotional. The emotional target is focused and readable without becoming cold: players should feel the weight of the game while still getting plain instructions, clear status language, and straightforward labels that work for casual friend-group play.

### Aesthetic Direction
The visual direction should lean into historical diplomacy aesthetics in light mode. Think war-room paper, lacquered tokens, archival maps, field manuals, seal stamps, and restrained ceremonial detail rather than neon, esports, or abstract startup minimalism. The interface should prioritize clarity on phones, but keep enough texture and gravitas that the game feels consequential when opened for a 20-second check-in. No explicit references were provided, so the design should avoid generic shadcn-neutral dashboard styling and instead build a distinctive tabletop-strategy atmosphere.

### Design Principles
1. Phone-first command clarity. The current phase, required action, and room state should be legible within seconds on a small screen.
2. Historical atmosphere without clutter. Use materials, typography, and color cues that suggest diplomatic documents and campaign maps, but keep interactions clean and fast.
3. Serious, not sterile. The product should feel strategic and weighty, while still welcoming casual friend groups rather than competitive hardcore players only.
4. Light surfaces with tonal depth. Prefer parchment, ink, brass, oxblood, naval blue, and muted military hues over bright gamey colors or monochrome SaaS neutrals.
5. Operational copy over marketing copy. Labels, helper text, empty states, and status messages should explain what the user can do or what changed. Avoid slogans, hype, self-congratulation, and product-showcase language.
6. Every screen should reinforce consequence through structure, not copywriting. Joining a room, choosing a power, reviewing results, and submitting orders should feel meaningful because the interface is clear and deliberate, not because the text is dramatic.
